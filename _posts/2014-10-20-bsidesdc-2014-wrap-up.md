BSidesDC holds a special place for me as I am one of the organizers of the conference. This year I stepped up more and chaired the CFP process, so I oversaw the submissions, coordinated with the evaluation team and worked with the speakers to let them know the status of their submissions and whether or not they would be speaking at the conference. There was a great deal of work that went into putting together another highly successful conference. We had almost 750 overall attendees (including speakers and sponsors), which made this a large endeavor by everybody involved in putting this conference together. My hats go off to everybody involved, but a huge thanks to Alex Normann and Mark Boltz who are the co-directors of the conference, and managed to keep us all in line throughout the weekend.

Like all conferences, this wasn't so much an opportunity to go see as many talks as possible, but rather an opportunity to network, meet new people and mingle with friends. I was however, able to see a few talks.

- Keynote - Jericho from [attrition.org][attrition]{:target="_blank"} ([@attritionorg][attritionorg]{:target="_blank"})  
 Jericho brings a depth of knowledge to the infosec field that is truly astounding. He has been involved in infosec since the old days before the term infosec even existed. He has a passion and drive to the field that we should all strive to match. A large part of the keynote was how much of an honor it was to be invited and how it was not something to take lightly, but rather something that would push the agenda of the entire conference, and he did not disappoint. I think the highlight of this talk, for me at least, was when Jericho gave all us defenders a nod discussing how it is difficult to do defense (no, I'm not saying offense is easy....we all know shit is difficult no matter what side you are on). As a defender and incident responder, this was awesome to hear. That said....the path forward is nothing simple. There were statistics listed on the number of disclosed vulnerabilities and the software involved that makes things very difficult for us defenders. That said...move forward and keep on doing what you do!

- Training with Raspberry Pi - Nathaniel Davis [(@Githur][githur]{:target="_blank"})  
 Githur will give me and other friends credit for convincing him to submit his original talk to BSidesLV, but the truth of the matter is that he created an amazing tool with a huge amount of potential for infosec professionals. All any of us did was push him to make the strive to provide the community with the opportunity to see what his hard work has matured into. Githur is going to make big things happen for himself, and for infosec, and I just hope he remembers the little people when he's ruling the world. This talk was about the Raspberry Pi wireless training device he created. Githur took a RasPi and turned it into a wireless hacking platform. When the device is booted, it creates a wireless access point with a randomly generated WEP key that you have to crack in order to get access to the device. I know what you're thinking...WEP is outdated and easy to crack...well, you're not wrong; however, this is a training platform. Githur has plans to push out options for WPA and stronger encryption, but you have to start a tool like this somewhere and I think starting with WEP was the smartest to do it. He discussed the challenges he ran into during the process of the tool and even made his code available on [sourceforge][rangebox]{:target="_blank"} for anybody to download and run or play around with. I suggest you go check it out and give him some serious props for this original, and well built, training platform.

- Why I Sold Out, Why You Should Too - Karlo Arozqueta [(@ViciousData][viciousdata])  
 Karlo, aka #SalesPistols, talk was about how he made the switch from operations, and working in a SOC environment, to pre-sales engineer. This talk was hugely entertaining and Karlo is a great public speaker. This talk was detailed in the advantages to making the switch to pre-sales and the process that he took, why he made the shift and why he thinks some of us technical operations analysts might be considering a similar change. If you are thinking you're done with operations work and looking towards the next progression in your career, or just fed up with the bullshit in SOC life, this would be an excellent talk to check out. Hell, even if you are just looking for a really good laugh or what life on the dark side of the force is like, check out this talk. It was great.

Those were the only talks I got a chance to see live, but from what I've heard from the attendees, they were very happy with the lineup of topics presented, so if you have a chance, go check them out when they are released. They were all filmed, so they should be coming out in the coming weeks.

As for me, the highlight of the conference was definitely Saturday night. The official conference party was at the Iron Horse in downtown DC. Drinks were flowing and I was able to meet some new people, but more than anything, I was able to sit down and catch up with some friends. Huge props to Jez ([@0daypizza][0daypizza]{:target="_blank"}) and PJ ([@pjhinders][pjhinders]{:target="_blank"}) who managed to get me on a dance floor completely sober. There are people who can't get me on the dance floor drunk, so the fact they were able to do it while I was sober is a special feat. Throughout the night, we laughed, we joked, we danced. Not sure what else could be asked for on a night out on the town.

Now it's time to look towards BSidesDC 2015!
