Ever since I was a kid, I have had problems with my self image. Growing up I was always at least a little overweight. It has lead to periods of self-induced seclusion and depressive periods. I won't say depression outright, because that is a terrible mental health issue that affects many people I know, but depressive periods. I have a larger frame which didn't help, and I've had some medical issues that certainly made things worse, but the blame falls on myself. I have a nasty sweet tooth (fruity candies are my jam) and love to eat foods that are bad for me. Looking at you pizza and pasta. This is my biggest downfall and something I fight with to this day. I have made huge strides in recent years in my overall fitness, but also in my nutrition. 

A little history lesson on how I got here. Prior to recently, my senior year in high school remained as the healthiest time of my life. I would wake up and do cardio workouts with my brother at 530am, 3 times per week, before class, and would do weightlifting with my best friend after we got out of class. This all happened before I was diagnosed with Ulcerative Colitis the week before I graduated high school. In fact, I was hospitalized the days before my graduation ceremony and was released from the hospital the day before the graduation ceremony. I could barely walk to receive my diploma. I spent six years on and off a host of drugs prescribed by my doctors. The worst of these included a steroid called Prednisone. If you've never heard of Prednisone, you are lucky. It is easily the worst drug I have ever been on. Common side effects include kidney damage, liver damage, and extreme weight gain. At my heaviest, during my time overseas in Kuwait, I was pushing 250-lbs. 

After six years I decided the drugs weren't providing long term remission and spoke with a surgeon, who agreed with my assessment. I had a major surgery in October of 2009 to have my large intestine completely removed. This was actually a two part surgery and I spent six weeks on long term disability from work. During this time, I did not eat, I did not exercise, I could barely walk up the stairs. I completely acknowledge the only reason I am even alive right now is because of my roommate at the time and best friend, Katie, who would force me to eat dinner every night. I think at my least healthy I weighed in at, or around, 145-lbs. Following the second surgery I was able to begin eating normally again and return to work.

Now I wish I had known about CrossFit at this time. Starting out as weak as I was, it would've been nice to be able to jump right into a workout regimen that I have grown to love. Instead I spent the next four years trying out various Globo-Gyms, quitting, signing up again, quitting after a couple months, rinse and repeat. It wasn't until I moved to Charleston that I really got into CrossFit and started to love it. At this point, I wasn't very healthy. I smoked, I drank a lot. Living in DC will do that to you. I started going a few days a week and have since pushed it to closer to six days a week, often doing 2x/day workouts. That said, I still wasn't healthy. My nutrition was still bad....like really bad. It was time to make a change.

![]({{ site.baseimg }}/img/steps.jpg){:style="display: block; margin: 0 auto; width: 50%;"}

I decided that for the [2017 CrossFit Open]({{ "/2017-02-22-i-am-intheopen/" | relative_url }}), I was going to spend five weeks eating extremely healthy, no cheat meals, and no alcohol. It was five weeks, how hard could it be? As it turns out, harder than you would think. During this time, we celebrated my niece's 4th birthday. This meant dinner at Mellow Mushroom, where I got to see everybody else enjoy amazing pizza while I ate a "build your own" salad. Then we went to her grandmother's house for cake, to watch everybody enjoy what seemed to be an extremely good cake. I had nights where I went out with friends and couldn't partake in shenanigans because of my strict diet. I had to give up lunches, and dinners, with coworkers because it didn't fit my meal plan. This was all something I chose to sacrifice. Looking back I don't regret it, as I've made gains (I guess technically losses) that fit my goals. While it was difficult at the time, I realize it wasn't even really that difficult as long as my mindset was simple. Do this for the short term in order to get long term results.

In my five week test, I failed. I had two cheat meals. And both times I did, I woke up the next day and immediately felt the regret of cheating. I can say it was because I was sick, with a migraine and light-headed, but I'd be lying. I could have stuck to my meal plan through that. In reality, it was a short term weakness in my otherwise iron clad fortitude. In the end I got the results I was looking for, down 20-lbs and 3% body fat. I will continue to have a clean nutrition plan, with the occasional cheat based on events. I won't be afraid to go out with friends and have a couple drinks, or go to lunch with coworkers. I will, however, continue to evolve; discover what works for me and progress toward that end goal. There will be cheat days, there will be setbacks, but I will not allow myself to fail.

![]({{ site.baseimg }}/img/self-esteem.jpg){:style="display: block; margin: 0 auto; width: 75%;"}

I am investing in myself. Over the last five weeks I have learned a lot. It's not easy to give up the things you enjoy. To give up cake with your niece on her birthday, or dinner with friends and family. These short term pleasures that we have to give up help us to achieve long term success. My only advice is to invest in yourself. If there is something you don't like, only you can implement what needs to be done in order to change your situation. It won't be easy, and there will be setbacks, but never quit and continue to evolve. You will surprise yourself.
